What is Root Canal Treatment?

Root Canal Treatment is a procedure performed to remove infected or inflamed pulp tissue from inside a tooth and preserve the natural tooth instead of extracting it.

The pulp contains nerves, blood vessels, and connective tissue. When infection reaches the pulp due to deep decay, trauma, or cracks, RCT becomes necessary.

When is RCT Required?

  • Severe spontaneous tooth pain
  • Pain while chewing
  • Prolonged sensitivity to hot or cold
  • Swelling in gums
  • Pus discharge
  • Infection visible on X-ray

What is a “Hot Tooth”?

A hot tooth refers to a tooth with irreversible pulpitis that is extremely painful and difficult to anesthetize.

  • Severe throbbing pain
  • Pain increases with heat
  • Temporary relief with cold water
  • Common in lower molars
  • Standard anesthesia may not work easily

Why anesthesia fails: Inflamed tissues have lower pH, which reduces the effectiveness of local anesthetic. Nerve fibers are also hyperactive.

Management techniques include:

  • Supplemental injections
  • Intraligamentary injection
  • Intraosseous injection
  • Intrapulpal injection
  • Preoperative pain medication

Step-by-Step RCT Procedure

1. Diagnosis

Clinical examination, percussion test, thermal test, and X-ray evaluation are performed to confirm the diagnosis.

2. Local Anesthesia

The tooth is numbed to ensure a painless procedure. Hot tooth cases may require additional techniques.

3. Isolation

A rubber dam is placed to maintain a sterile and dry working field.

4. Access Opening

A small opening is made in the crown to access the pulp chamber.

5. Cleaning & Shaping

Infected pulp is removed and canals are cleaned using rotary or hand files along with disinfecting solutions.

6. Obturation

The canals are sealed using gutta-percha and sealer to prevent reinfection.

7. Crown Placement

Most back teeth require a crown after RCT to prevent fracture and ensure long-term success.

How Many Visits Are Required?

Depending on infection severity, RCT may be completed in one visit or may require 2–3 visits.

Possible Complications

  • Post-operative discomfort
  • Instrument separation
  • Missed canal
  • Reinfection
  • Tooth fracture without crown

Is RCT Painful?

Modern RCT is generally painless. Pain is usually caused by infection, not the treatment itself.

Success rate of RCT is approximately 85–95% when performed under proper sterile conditions and followed by crown placement.
